## 1. Feasibility verdict

**YES — with conditions.** Relicensing Multiview from `MIT OR Apache-2.0` to a source-available "free for non-commercial, paid for commercial" model is entirely feasible, and your timing is ideal.

**Recommended approach (one paragraph):** Adopt a **dual-license** model. Do this **before the first public tag**, put a **CLA** in place so you can lawfully sell commercial licenses over the whole codebase, keep `gpl-codecs` strictly opt-in (it can never ship under the non-commercial license), and **stop calling the project "open source."**

The four genuine conditions: **(1)** you must own/control 100% of the copyright (true today if you're sole author; a CLA is required before any outside contribution); **(2)** you must relicense *before* first public release; **(3)** the `gpl-codecs` GPL build is hard-incompatible and must never be distributed under the non-commercial terms; **(4)** a lawyer must draft the bespoke definitions.

## 2. Can you even do this?

**The core principle (fact).** A copyright owner may license the *same* work under as many different sets of terms as they wish, and may change the terms offered for *future* copies at any time. What they cannot do is retroactively cancel a license grant already validly made to someone who already received the code.

- **Apache-2.0 §2/§3** grant an explicitly "perpetual … irrevocable" copyright **and patent** license to recipients — so for Apache-licensed code the non-revocability is airtight on the face of the text ([Apache 2.0](https://www.apache.org/licenses/LICENSE-2.0)).
- **MIT** does **not** contain the word "irrevocable." It is *treated* as non-revocable for code already received under it — the rights "continue to apply to that code even if future versions are released under a different license" — but that conclusion rests on consideration/estoppel rather than an express grant, and is the **strong majority view rather than a settled certainty**: some authors have publicly attempted to "revoke" MIT grants (e.g. the Hangfire, IdentityServer, and FluentAssertions relicensing disputes), which the prevailing view treats as ineffective against copies already distributed ([OSI MIT](https://opensource.org/license/mit); [Wikipedia: MIT License](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIT_License)).

**What this means for you:**

1. You **cannot** claw back the permissive grant on any copy already distributed. For **Apache-2.0** code this is certain ("irrevocable"). For **MIT** code it is the strong majority position (resting on consideration/estoppel) rather than as airtight as Apache's express grant — but the practical answer is the same: whoever lawfully holds such a copy keeps permissive rights to *that* code. (This is moot for you in practice because the repo is not yet public.)
2. You **can** release all future versions (and re-release current code as a fresh version) under any new license you choose, **provided you own/control the copyright in all of it**.
3. **Your decisive advantage: the repo is not yet public.** No public permissive copies are outstanding, so there is no downstream community with vested MIT/Apache rights to a public version. **Relicense before the first public tag.** After even a single public release, anyone can fork *that* version and continue it under its original terms forever. Recent precedents illustrate the dynamic — but note the prior licenses differed: **OpenSearch** forked **Elasticsearch** from its **Apache-2.0 (permissive)** era; **Valkey** forked **Redis** from its **BSD-3 (permissive)** era; **OpenTofu** forked **Terraform** from its **MPL-2.0 (weak-copyleft)** era. All three confirm the general rule (a copy already distributed can be forked and continued under its then-license), even though MPL-2.0 is weak copyleft rather than permissive. **(Opinion, strongly held.)**

**Contributors / inbound IP — required to SELL commercial licenses.** To dual-license you must be able to license the *entire* codebase under proprietary commercial terms — which you can only do for code you **own** or for which you hold an explicit **right to relicense commercially**. An inbound permissive contribution (MIT/Apache) lets you *use and redistribute* it but does **not** let you sublicense it under new proprietary terms ([TermsFeed: dual licensing](https://www.termsfeed.com/blog/dual-license-open-source-commercial/)).

| Inbound mechanism | Lets you dual-license / sell commercial? |
|---|---|
| **DCO** (`Signed-off-by`) — your repo's current model | **No.** Certifies right-to-submit; transfers no relicensing rights ([Kemitchell: DCO is not a CLA](https://writing.kemitchell.com/2021/07/02/DCO-Not-CLA)) |
| **CLA** (contributor keeps copyright, grants you broad rights incl. relicense/commercial) | **Yes** — the standard tool for dual-license/open-core ([opensource.com: CLA vs DCO](https://opensource.com/article/18/3/cla-vs-dco-whats-difference)) |
| **Copyright assignment** | Yes (maximal), but highest contributor friction |

**Recommendation:** Adopt a **CLA**, not bare DCO and not full assignment — an **Apache-ICLA-style** agreement augmented with an explicit "inbound = outbound, **plus** the right to license under other terms including commercial/proprietary." Automate it with **CLA Assistant** ([github.com/cla-assistant](https://github.com/cla-assistant/cla-assistant)) gating PRs. This is **non-negotiable** for the commercial-sale side and must be in place *before* you accept any outside contribution. Today, if you are the sole author, you already hold all rights — so the urgent action is "CLA before the first external PR," not retroactive cleanup.

**Third-party code already in the tree** stays usable (permissive licenses allow redistribution inside a larger source-available work) — but you **cannot** sell a commercial license over *that* code as if it were yours; the upstream terms continue to govern it. Maintain a `THIRD-PARTY-NOTICES` manifest (generate with `cargo-about`) and scope your commercial license to *your* code + a pass-through of upstream permissive terms.

## 3. What kind of license this is (and isn't)

**This is a *source-available* license. It is NOT open source and NOT free software. This is a hard, factual constraint, not stylistic.**

The reasons to drop "open source" are, in priority order:

1. **(Strongest — definitional fact.) OSD §5 — No Discrimination Against Persons or Groups (verbatim):** *"The license must not discriminate against any person or group of persons."* Your commercial gate + exceptions treat classes of users differently → fails §5.
2. **OSD §6 — No Discrimination Against Fields of Endeavor (verbatim):** *"The license must not restrict anyone from making use of the program in a specific field of endeavor. For example, it may not restrict the program from being used in a business, or from being used for genetic research."* A non-commercial-only license does exactly the forbidden thing → fails §6. (Both verified at [opensource.org/osd](https://opensource.org/osd).)
3. **Not "free software" either:** FSF Freedom 0 is *"the freedom to run the program as you wish, for any purpose,"* and the FSF states plainly *"'Free software' does not mean 'noncommercial'"* ([gnu.org/philosophy/free-sw](https://www.gnu.org/philosophy/free-sw.html)).
4. **Tooling / community hygiene:** SPDX, package registries, and the wider community treat "open source" as the OSI-approved set; misusing it invites the SSPL/Elastic and HashiCorp-BSL style backlash.
5. **(Weakest — speculative, for counsel.) Possible Australian Consumer Law angle:** describing a non-OSI license as "open source" *might* be argued to be misleading or deceptive conduct under ACL s18 if it materially affects a purchasing decision. This is **untested and speculative** — raise it with counsel as a tail risk, **not** as a primary driver. The definitional point (reasons 1–3) is the real reason. **(Opinion.)**

**Action:** Use **"source-available"** consistently — optionally qualified as **"source-available, free for non-commercial use."** **Scrub every "open source" / "FOSS" / "OSS" reference** from the repo, docs, README, badges, web SPA, and package metadata (see §8). Keep "LGPL-clean" only as a factual statement about *FFmpeg linkage* — but stop describing *Multiview itself* as open source.

## 4. Recommended structure — the dual-license model + base instrument

**The model (fact).** Dual licensing offers the *same* codebase under two parallel sets of terms: a restrictive public license **and** a paid commercial license that lifts the restriction ([TermsFeed: dual licensing vs open core](https://www.termsfeed.com/blog/dual-licensing-vs-open-core/)). Your three exceptions are simply **additional free "Permitted Purpose" grants written into the public license** — extra free lanes, not separate agreements. Proven by MySQL (the original dual-license), Qt, **Sidekiq** (a *solo-owner* seven-figure proof point — [Commercial FAQ](https://github.com/sidekiq/sidekiq/wiki/Commercial-FAQ)), iText, and Ghostscript/Artifex (which *litigated and won* against an unlicensed commercial bundler).

### Base-instrument comparison

| Instrument | Free-for-noncommercial fit | Custom carve-out slot? | Auto-open-sources? | Education/Gov default | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PolyForm Noncommercial 1.0.0** | **Excellent** (purpose-built) | No native slot — edit "Permitted Purpose" | **No** | **FREE** (must override) | **RECOMMENDED base.** SPDX-listed, lawyer-drafted, plain-language, 32-day cure, no mandatory open-sourcing ([text](https://polyformproject.org/licenses/noncommercial/1.0.0)) |
| **BUSL/BSL 1.1** | Poor as-is (gates *production*, not commercial) | **Yes** — free-form "Additional Use Grant" | **Yes, mandatory** (GPL-compatible Change License, ≤4 yrs) | n/a | **Structural inspiration**, not base — its Additional-Use-Grant mechanism is the model for embedding arbitrary policy, but its mandatory eventual GPL conversion is a poor fit if you never want to auto-open-source ([mariadb.com/bsl11](https://mariadb.com/bsl11/)) |
| **FSL (Sentry)** | Anti-fit — restricts only *competing* use; allows ordinary commercial | No | Yes (MIT/Apache after 2 yrs) | n/a | **Reject** — wrong restriction axis ([fsl.software](https://fsl.software/)) |
| **Prosperity 3.0.0** | Good home/hobby half | No | No | **FREE** (same flaw as PolyForm) | Partial — same family as PolyForm, same education/gov problem ([text](https://prosperitylicense.com/versions/3.0.0)) |
| **CC-BY-NC 4.0** | n/a | No | No | Neutral, but vague | **Reject** — Creative Commons *officially recommends against CC for software* ([CC FAQ](https://creativecommons.org/faq/)); "NonCommercial" is a vague purpose test |
| **Fully bespoke proprietary** | Perfect | Total | Your choice | Your choice | **Fallback only** — encodes anything, but highest legal cost, no SPDX id, no body of interpretation, least recognition |

**Recommendation: build on the PolyForm Noncommercial 1.0.0 skeleton, with a lawyer-rewritten "Permitted Purpose," paired with a separate commercial license.** Rationale: PolyForm is purpose-built for *exactly* "free non-commercial / pay commercial," is plain-language and lawyer-drafted, ships a clean **32-day cure-then-terminate** and fair-use savings clause, has **no mandatory eventual open-sourcing** (unlike BSL), and — critically — **`PolyForm-Noncommercial-1.0.0` is on the SPDX License List** ([spdx.org](https://spdx.org/licenses/PolyForm-Noncommercial-1.0.0.html)), which keeps tooling sane. Because your three carve-outs and the education/government reversal require *editing the "Permitted Purpose" definition*, the result is a **derived/bespoke license** (it can no longer claim the pure `PolyForm-Noncommercial-1.0.0` SPDX id once modified) — but the PolyForm skeleton gives you a tested chassis around your custom policy. Borrow the **revenue-threshold drafting pattern from PolyForm Small Business** ("less than 1,000,000 USD … total revenue in the prior tax year," CPI-adjusted — [text](https://polyformproject.org/licenses/small-business/1.0.0/)) for the community-broadcaster cap.

> **Drafting-model decision to put to counsel (structural, important).** PolyForm is deliberately a **bare copyright license**: it imposes *conditions on a grant*, and using the work outside those conditions is **copyright infringement** — which is precisely what gives you the strong "infringement, not mere breach" enforcement lever in §9 (injunctions, statutory/actual damages). The draft below adds acceptance language (§1), a warranty disclaimer and liability cap (§13), and governing-law / ACL-savings clauses (§14) — all of which push the instrument toward a **contract**. The risk: a court may then characterise a violation as *contract breach* (weaker remedy, no statutory damages) rather than infringement, and may require offer/acceptance/consideration formation against users who never signed. This is a genuine trade-off, not a drafting accident: PolyForm's strength is the bare-license framing, and bolting on contract machinery has a cost. **Have counsel decide deliberately which model you want, and keep the chosen drafting internally consistent with it.**

**Go fully bespoke only if** you affirmatively want a different chassis; **consider BSL's Additional-Use-Grant mechanism only if** you are comfortable with mandatory eventual conversion to a GPL-compatible license (a real decision point — see §10).

## 5. Defining "commercial" vs "non-commercial" + the three exceptions

**The single most important finding:** the two best-known "non-commercial" licenses do the **opposite** of what you want for education and government — they make them **FREE**.

- **PolyForm Noncommercial 1.0.0 (verbatim):** *"Use by any charitable organization, **educational institution**, public research organization, public safety or health organization, environmental protection organization, or **government institution** is use for a permitted purpose regardless of the source of funding…"* ([source](https://polyformproject.org/licenses/noncommercial/1.0.0)). The forthcoming 2.0.0-pre.2 keeps the same stance.
- **CC-BY-NC** is a *purpose* test, not a *status* test; CC's own guidance: *"no class of reuser is per se permitted or excluded"* ([CC NC interpretation](https://wiki.creativecommons.org/wiki/NonCommercial_interpretation)).

**Consequence:** You **cannot** adopt any stock noncommercial license unmodified, **cannot** name your license "Noncommercial," and **must define "commercial use" affirmatively**, naming the surprising inclusions out loud.

### Make education & government explicitly commercial

Lead with the rule, then a non-exhaustive "includes, without limitation" list that *names* business, education (institution + staff/students acting in that capacity), government (all levels), creator/streamer use, and appliance/productization. Add a **recital stating intent**: that education and government use is deliberately commercial here, departing from other "non-commercial" licenses, so any industry custom to the contrary does not apply. A court construing ambiguity looks to stated intent. **(Opinion.)** Take care to let a teacher's genuine *at-home hobby* use ride the personal grant — define education as use *"by, for, or in connection with the activities of"* an institution, not "any use by anyone who is a teacher."

### Scope the personal grant by capacity, not status (fixes the sole-trader gap)

There is a latent contradiction to resolve **in the operative text, not just prose**: a sole trader is a natural person, so a sole trader's genuine at-home hobby use could fall under *both* the personal grant *and* the "sole trader" inclusion of Commercial Use. **Fix:** tie the personal grant to the **capacity** of the use — *"use unconnected with any trade, business, or profession of the user"* — rather than the **status** of the person, and make the "sole trader" inclusion reach **only** use *in or for* that trade/business. This keeps a large, sympathetic user class (sole traders tinkering at home) on the free side while still charging genuine business use. The draft below applies this fix.

### Why "commercial" is genuinely hard

CC's 2009 "Defining Noncommercial" study (Mellon-funded) found broad agreement at the extremes (money-making ≈ 89–92/100; private use ≈ 16–24) but **consensus collapsed on the edges you care about** — nonprofit cost-recovery scored ~59 (creators) vs ~72 (users) ([report](https://creativecommons.org/2009/09/14/defining-noncommercial-report-published/)). **Bright-line numeric tests (dollar caps, subscriber counts) are far more defensible than a purpose test — lean into them.**

**The *Great Minds v. FedEx Office* lesson (886 F.3d 91, 2d Cir. 2018):** a noncommercial licensee was held free to use a *commercial agent* (FedEx) to exercise its own rights, because the license didn't expressly forbid it — **silence is read in the user's favor** ([Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca2/17-808/17-808-2018-03-21.html)). **Drafting fix:** every exception must carry an anti-agency rider — the exempt user's *own* activities only; no commercial integrator/contractor may shelter under the exception to run the software as a commercial service.

### The three free exceptions — defensible tests (with residual fact-intensity flagged)

Architecture for each: **defined term + bright-line eligibility test + anti-abuse rider + self-certification hook.** A caution before the detail: the *numeric* tests (dollar cap, audience count) are genuinely bright-line; the *control* and *group* tests below necessarily reintroduce **fact-intensive inquiry** (effective control, decentralised brands) — the very thing bright lines were meant to avoid. Flag these to counsel; they are the soft spots, not self-executing.

**Exception 1 — First Nations Owned Broadcaster.** Anchor in existing, objective machinery rather than asking the *Licensor* to adjudicate Indigeneity. Two-limb "**registry OR ownership-and-control**" test:
- *Registry limb:* registered with ORIC under the *Corporations (Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander) Act 2006* (Cth); OR holds an Australian community broadcasting licence allocated to an Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander community interest; OR is a member of **First Nations Media Australia** ([firstnationsmedia.org.au](https://firstnationsmedia.org.au/our-industry/radio-services)); OR, outside Australia, is recognised by the relevant national Indigenous broadcasting body/regulator (iwi radio / Whakaata Māori in NZ; CRTC-recognised in Canada; FCC Tribal licensee in the US).
- *Ownership-and-control limb:* **more than 50%** of *both* beneficial ownership *and* the governing body are First Nations persons, **and** not controlled by any non-First-Nations person — mirroring CATSI's rule that **50/50 control is treated as *non*-Indigenous** ([ORIC Indigeneity requirement](https://www.oric.gov.au/for-corporations/corporate-governance/indigeneity-requirement)). The "more than half of *both*" test blocks a 49% Indigenous "front."
- **[UNCERTAIN — residual loophole]** The ownership-and-control limb still permits a non-First-Nations party to hold **effective control through financing, IP licensing, or contractual veto** without owning >50% or sitting on the board. The "not controlled, directly or indirectly, by any non-First-Nations person" catch-all helps — but that is exactly the **fact-heavy control test** that undercuts the "bright-line" selling point. Ask counsel to add a **"control in substance"** definition that *names* financing, IP, and veto control. Separately, who is "First Nations" is contested and politically sensitive; the registry limb (objective, third-party) is far safer than asking you to judge ancestry. Lawyer must confirm the cross-jurisdiction wording is respectful and accurate.

**Exception 2 — Community Broadcaster with < USD $1,000,000 sponsorship/yr.** Australia already regulates both concepts:
- "Community broadcasting licence" is a statutory class under the **Broadcasting Services Act 1992 (Cth) s 84**, requiring **not-for-profit** operation and no government/political control ([AustLII s 84](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/cth/consol_act/bsa1992214/s84.html)).
- **"Sponsorship" is a distinct, regulated revenue line — NOT "advertising"** (community stations may broadcast tagged sponsorship announcements; advertising is prohibited — [CBAA](https://www.cbaa.org.au/resources/resource-library/sponsorship)). The hourly cap differs by medium and — relevant here, since Multiview is a **video** multiviewer and its community-broadcaster users will overwhelmingly be **community-TV** licensees — the correct figure is **7 minutes/hour for community television** (community radio is 5 minutes/hour), per Broadcasting Services Act 1992 Sch 2 and the ACMA Community Broadcasting Sponsorship Guidelines ([ACMA: ad time limits](https://www.acma.gov.au/ad-time-limits); [ACMA Guidelines 2008](https://www.acma.gov.au/sites/default/files/2019-06/Community-Broadcasting-Sponsorship-Guidelines-2008.pdf)). **Note:** the hourly cap is incidental colour only — your auditable **dollar-meter is gross sponsorship *revenue*, not airtime** — so do not over-weight it; if cited in the license, cite the 7-min TV figure as primary.
- Defaults to set: **gross** (not net) sponsorship revenue; measured over the **most recently completed financial year**; **aggregated across related/commonly-controlled entities** (anti-shunting); **substance-over-form** (counts "sponsorship in substance" even if relabeled "underwriting"/"partnership"); converted to USD at the **central-bank rate on the financial-year-end date**.
- **[UNCERTAIN]** Non-Australian "community broadcaster" definitions vary; the "or equivalent licence class issued by a national regulator" catch-all needs a lawyer's eye so it isn't so loose any low-power FM qualifies.

**Exception 3 — Content Creator with < 55,000 subscribers across ALL channels.** Hardest to make bright-line because "subscriber" differs per platform. Define a single normalized **"Audience Count"** and a **summation rule**:
- *Audience Count* = the **simple arithmetic sum across all the user's Channels** of the largest native audience figure each platform reports (subscribers/followers/members; newsletter subscribers; for podcasts, average unique downloads/listeners per episode over trailing 90 days). **No cross-platform de-duplication.**
- **Be honest about what no-dedup does (policy choice, not a neutral anti-abuse win).** No-dedup is simpler and auditable, but it is a **deliberate policy choice that errs toward paid** — it can push a genuinely small creator (e.g. five platforms × 12k true-but-overlapping followers = 60k counted) into the paid tier even though their *unique* reach is under 55,000, while a 50k single-platform creator rides free. That is **not unambiguously "in your favour"**: it charges someone who is, in substance, exactly who you meant to exempt. Two consequences for counsel: (i) a consumer-facing ambiguity may be read **contra proferentem** (against you, the drafter) and — echoing the *Great Minds* "silence for the user" instinct — a court could read the no-dedup rule *down*; (ii) for a standard-form consumer contract, confirm the no-dedup term is **not an "unfair contract term"** under the ACL unfair-terms regime. Keep no-dedup if you want simplicity, but state plainly that it is intentional, and consider whether the **secondary revenue gate** (below) is the better discriminator of "who we mean to exempt."
- *Whose channels:* the natural person **plus channels under their control or operated in their name** (blocks splitting one brand across ten "separate" sub-55k channels). **[UNCERTAIN — residual loophole]** The "group of natural persons acting together" + "channels in their name" wording still leaves a media company able to operate through nominal individual "creators," each under 55k, in a **genuinely decentralised brand** that the anti-conduit (agency) rule does not reach. Ask counsel for an explicit **aggregation rule for commonly-controlled creator entities/brands**, in addition to the anti-agency rider.
- *Measurement:* re-test at first use and quarterly.
- **[OPERATOR DECISION]** Consider a *secondary revenue gate* (the exception does not apply if the creator earns more than $X/yr from the content, even if under 55k) — a tiny channel pulling large brand-deal money is arguably not who you mean to exempt. This also doubles as a cleaner discriminator than no-dedup for the over-count edge case. Adds complexity; your call.

### Grow-out / threshold-crossing rule (uniform, all numeric exceptions)

> If a user qualifies and later ceases to qualify, they may continue using **the version(s) then in use** under the exception for a **90-day transition period**, after which continued use — and any upgrade or new installation — requires a commercial license. Crossing back under a threshold later does not retroactively restore the exception.

This is humane (no "you tripped to 55,001 mid-stream and are now an infringer"), rewards early hobbyist adopters (your growth engine), and — by limiting the grandfather to the *specific version* and excluding upgrades — avoids a perpetual free-use revenue leak. **[OPERATOR DECISION]** how generous the grandfather is.

### Cross-cutting machinery (write once, globally)

- **Good-faith self-certification + audit:** eligibility rests on the user's good-faith self-certification as at the measurement date; you may, on (e.g.) 14 days' notice and no more than annually, request reasonable evidence (registry records, the relevant revenue line, native platform dashboards); material misrepresentation = commercial use without a license from the date it began, plus back-fees. Pair with the **32-day cure** so honest users can fix or buy before termination.
- **Anti-agency (Great Minds):** stated once globally — no exempt user may be a conduit so a non-exempt party gets commercial value.
- **Anti-relabeling:** substance over form for revenue, audience, and entity-type tests.
- **Severability that fails *safe* (toward paid):** if any exception or external-statute reference is held invalid, it is read down or severed, and the invalidity of an exception **does not** invalidate the default rule that commercial use requires a commercial license.
- **Governing law:** **[UNCERTAIN]** Australian law is the natural fit given the Australian-anchored definitions; confirm enforceability against overseas users.

## 6. ⚠️ Dependency compatibility — the critical section (the GPL crux)

| Dependency class | In default build? | Compatible with a non-commercial license on *your* code? | What you must do |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Permissive** (MIT/Apache/BSD/ISC/Zlib/Unicode/CC0) | Yes (bulk) | **YES** | Preserve copyright + license notices; carry Apache `NOTICE` + note any modifications to Apache files; **don't fork-and-edit MPL-2.0 files in place** (MPL is per-file copyleft) |
| **LGPL-2.1 FFmpeg** (default) + GStreamer/GLib (optional `rtsp-server`) | Yes (default) | **YES** | See conditions below |
| **GPLv2 x264/x265 via `gpl-codecs`** | **No (opt-in only)** | **NO — HARD INCOMPATIBILITY** | Never ship under the non-commercial license; see below |

**LGPL FFmpeg is fine — LGPL §6 *explicitly* permits the larger work under "terms of your choice"** (which includes non-commercial and proprietary terms), provided four conditions hold ([LGPL-2.1 §6](https://www.gnu.org/licenses/old-licenses/lgpl-2.1.txt)):
1. FFmpeg stays under LGPL (you don't relicense it; your terms govern only *your* code).
2. **The user can replace/relink the LGPL library** — satisfied automatically by **dynamic linking** (your LGPL-clean default; verified — Multiview links libav\* via `ffmpeg-sys-next`/`pkg-config` against the system shared libraries, and the deploy image ships `libav*.so.*` dynamically). **Mandate dynamic linking of all LGPL components** in shipped builds so this is trivially met. If you ever statically link FFmpeg (a single-binary "multiview box"), you then owe **§6(a)**: ship relinkable object files / the app in object form so a user can substitute a modified FFmpeg and relink — heavier, and it undercuts a closed appliance. Keep FFmpeg dynamic in appliances, or budget the object-file obligation.
3. Carry FFmpeg's LGPL-2.1 text + attribution; state the work uses the Library.
4. **⚠️ Your EULA must NOT forbid modification or reverse-engineering of the LGPL part.** LGPL §6 requires that your terms "permit modification of the work for the customer's own use and reverse engineering for debugging such modifications." A blanket "no reverse engineering / no modification" clause would **violate** LGPL §6. This is the single most-missed LGPL obligation for proprietary/NC larger works — **flag it explicitly to the lawyer** so the non-commercial *and* commercial EULAs carve out modify/RE-for-debug-and-relink of the FFmpeg portion.

**The hard wall — `gpl-codecs` (x264/x265, GPLv2) can NEVER ship under the non-commercial license:**
- **GPLv2 §2(b):** a combined/derivative work must be "licensed as a whole at no charge to all third parties under the terms of this License." On the **FSF's expansive reading of "derivative work"** — which x264 LLC itself adopts — **linking x264/x265 makes the whole build GPL.** Be precise about the epistemic status: whether *linking* (and especially *dynamic* linking) creates a single GPL "work as a whole" versus "mere aggregation" is a **genuinely unsettled question** that the FSF asserts expansively and that no high court has definitively decided. The practical conclusion is unchanged, and it is the **prudent assumption to build on** — but state it as the FSF/x264-LLC position rather than a judicially settled fact.
- **GPLv2 §6 / GPLv3 §10:** *"You may not impose any further restrictions on the … rights granted."* A "non-commercial only" clause is a quintessential further restriction — and contradicts Freedom 0 ("run … for any purpose"). ([GPLv2](https://www.gnu.org/licenses/old-licenses/gpl-2.0.en.html); [GPLv3](https://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-3.0.html))
- **Therefore the two licenses are mutually exclusive on the *same* artifact.** There is no drafting trick.

**Safe handling:**
1. Keep `gpl-codecs` strictly opt-in, off-by-default, and excluded from the cargo-deny allowlist (it already is). **Never** add x264/x265 to any default or shipped preset.
2. **Your release pipeline already enforces this — keep it that way.** Verified: the app-image CI (`docker.yml`) builds **only** `lgpl` and `nvidia` (still-LGPL) FFmpeg bases with features `ffmpeg,linux-vaapi,web` and `ffmpeg,nvidia,web` — never `gpl-codecs`. Cleanest: never distribute a `gpl-codecs` *application* binary at all — leave it as a source-only build flag the user enables themselves (a `CARGO_FEATURES=...,gpl-codecs` build is **user-self-service only**). **Audit note:** `ffmpeg-base.yml` separately builds and pushes a `multiview-ffmpeg:8.1.1-gpl` *base* image (x264/x265) to ghcr — that is FFmpeg-only, GPL upstream, and is **fine in itself** and is **not** consumed by the published Multiview app image in CI; the invariant to preserve is that this GPL base must **never** be combined with the published Multiview app image under NC terms. (If you ever did ship such a combined binary, that specific artifact would have to be offered under GPLv2 with full source and no NC restriction — defeating the monetization purpose.)
3. **A user who builds `gpl-codecs` themselves creates a GPL combined work that is realistically private-use-only** — they can run it, but on *redistribution* they hit the contradiction (your NC terms forbid the commercial redistribution; GPL forbids the NC restriction). Document this plainly.
4. **Commercial-codec alternative for paying H.264/H.265 customers:** a commercial Multiview license + the customer's *own* commercial codec license — x264 via x264 LLC, x265 via MulticoreWare ([x265 licensing](https://x265.com/about/license-x265-and-uhdcode-for-your-product-or-service/)), or MainConcept — keeps Multiview's distribution NC-clean. Note: H.264/H.265 use likely *also* needs a patent-pool license (MPEG-LA / Access Advance), independent of the software copyright license, landing on the commercial customer ([x264 licensing](https://x264.org/licensing/)).

**The one-line invariant to add to the codebase:** *"The non-commercial/commercial distribution links only permissive + dynamically-linked-LGPL code; `gpl-codecs` is never built into a distributed non-commercial/commercial artifact."*

> Note: this licensing change governs **your** code. It does **not** alter the FFmpeg/LGPL posture, the `gpl-codecs` opt-in, or NDI's separate proprietary runtime-load terms — those upstream component licenses you continue to comply with regardless.

## 7. Tooling impact

- **SPDX / `license` field:** Cargo validates `license` as an SPDX expression. `PolyForm-Noncommercial-1.0.0` *is* SPDX-listed — but once you **modify** the Permitted Purpose for your carve-outs, it's a **bespoke license with no SPDX id.** For a bespoke license, use **`license-file = "LICENSE"`**. Precision on Cargo's actual behaviour (verified): Cargo accepts `license`, `license-file`, **or both** (both together emits a *warning*, [cargo#8537](https://github.com/rust-lang/cargo/issues/8537)); crates.io requires **at least one**. Use `license-file` **alone** for a bespoke license — that avoids the warning and avoids asserting a non-existent SPDX id. On the much-discussed `LicenseRef-…` form: it is valid SPDX *expression syntax*, but **historically its handling on crates.io's manifest `license` validator has been unreliable**, and in any case this is **moot under your `publish = false` recommendation** — so don't state rejection as a present certainty; just use `license-file`.
- **crates.io:** your `release-plz.toml` already sets **`publish = false`** — crates never go to crates.io, so SPDX strictness is low-stakes. **Recommendation: keep it that way.** (Tooling clarification so the reason is *policy*, not impossibility: crates.io **can** publish a non-SPDX/NC license via `license-file` — it simply displays it as "non-standard"; the `LicenseRef-` issue is specific to the manifest `license` *expression* field. So `publish = false` is the right **policy choice**, not a tooling necessity.) Public crates.io publication of NC-licensed code is discouraged by registry norms (which assume redistributable open source). Each member crate is already `publish = false` (see §8).
- **cargo-deny:** the allowlist gates *dependency* licenses, not yours — no project-license change needed there. Note the workspace root is a **virtual manifest** (no root `[package]`), so there is no root crate license for cargo-deny to check; only the `publish = false` members exist, and `[licenses.private] ignore = true` skips license-checking *unpublished* workspace members (all of them, since all are `publish = false`). Keep `[graph] all-features = false` so `gpl-codecs`/GPL never enters the scanned graph — that's a desirable tripwire; preserve it. (Config verified at [cargo-deny docs](https://embarkstudios.github.io/cargo-deny/checks/licenses/cfg.html).)
- **`web/package.json`:** `"private": true`, no `license` field — nothing required, but add `"license": "SEE LICENSE IN LICENSE"` (npm's non-SPDX convention) for clarity.
- **`cargo deny` parse safety:** the root virtual manifest carries no `license`/`license-file`, so there is no SPDX-parse hazard at the root; member crates use `license-file.workspace = true` (see §8).

## 9. Enforcement & trademark

**Copyright is the legal lever (fact, with a drafting caveat).** A license is an enforceable conditional copyright grant; using the work outside the granted conditions (commercially when you only hold the non-commercial grant) is **copyright infringement** — supporting injunctions and statutory/actual damages, a stronger remedy than mere breach of contract. US courts enforce OSS license terms as copyright conditions (*Jacobsen v. Katzer*), and Artifex/Ghostscript litigated and prevailed against an unlicensed commercial bundler. **Caveat (cross-ref §4):** this strong "infringement, not breach" remedy depends on the instrument being characterised as a **bare copyright license** (conditions on a grant). The draft's added contract machinery (acceptance, liability cap, governing law, ACL clauses) risks pushing a court toward a **contract** characterisation, under which a violation is *breach* (weaker remedy). Have counsel keep the drafting consistent with the bare-license model if preserving this remedy matters.

**Detection is observational/reactive** (marketing "powered by Multiview," job ads, a competitor shipping a "multiview box," code/notice fingerprints, source-scanning tools). **Pursuit is cooperative-first, litigation-last** — the SFLC/Conservancy norm is that most violations are mistakes resolved privately; a polite "you appear to be using Multiview commercially — here's how to buy a license" converts most infringers into customers, and your **32-day cure window** structurally encourages this. **Practical limits:** internal/private commercial use is hard to see (enforcement realistically targets *visible* commercial use and *distribution* — the appliance/OEM case, where the money and brand harm are); cross-border enforcement from Australia is costly (budget "firm letters → convert to sales → litigate only flagrant high-value infringers"). The chief enforcement risk is **"commercial" ambiguity** — the strongest argument for the crisp, named, numeric definitions in §5.

**Trademark — your strongest *non-copyright* lever, but "Multiview" is a problem.** Trademark governs the brand independently of the code license: a free user may build/run the software but **cannot** use *your* registered brand to market a product (the Mozilla/Firefox and WordPress model). **But "Multiview"/"multiviewer" is an established generic industry term** for "a system that displays multiple A/V inputs as a mosaic" (used descriptively by TAG, Actus, DEXON, etc.), and IP Australia (Trade Marks Act 1995 s 41) refuses generic/descriptive marks. **A bare "Multiview" word mark is very likely refused or weak.** (Your codename "mosaic" is *also* a descriptive broadcast term — same weakness.)

**Recommendation:** (1) adopt a **coined/distinctive product name** under the **"aperim"** house mark (the "Kodak" strategy) — you can still *describe* the product as "a multiviewer" in prose (descriptive fair use). (2) Register the coined mark at **IP Australia** (home) + **USPTO** (US-heavy market), classes 9 (software) / 42 (SaaS) / possibly 38; consider the Madrid Protocol. (3) Publish a **Mozilla/WordPress-style trademark policy**: code is source-available under your license, but the name/logo are reserved — unauthorised/modified/commercial redistributions must be **unbranded** unless they hold a commercial+brand agreement. This makes "you may have the code, but you may not sell a *branded* Multiview box" enforceable independently of copyright.
